**Awareness stage:** Solution-aware with damage.
Maya has tried Goodreads, Discord, IRL clubs — all of them. She's not searching for a new app. She needs proof this is different at the mechanism level, not the feature level.

**Framework selected:** PAS (Problem → Agitate → Solve)
Best fit because: Maya is already problem-aware. Leading with pain she recognises gets her nodding before we make a claim.

**Messaging hierarchy:**
1. Primary: The conversation you've been missing isn't a forum — it's a reaction on the same page you're both on right now.
2. Supporting: Your club in under two minutes. No scheduling. No overhead.
3. Objection handler: This is different from what you've tried before — here's the mechanism.
4. CTA: Join the waitlist. Get your invite code before beta opens.

## 🔥 AGITATION SECTION

**Headline:**
You've felt this exact moment.

**Body:**
You're on chapter 11 of a book you love. Something just happened — a line that got under your skin, a plot turn you didn't see coming — and you want to say something about it *right now*, while the feeling is still there.

You pick up your phone. And then you remember: nobody you know is reading this book. By the time anyone is, the moment is gone. And so is the feeling that made reading feel alive.

You've tried to fix this. Goodreads. A Discord server that went quiet in week three. An IRL book club that couldn't survive three weeks of conflicting calendars. An app or two that promised "social reading" and delivered a feed.

None of them were wrong to try. They were just solving the wrong thing.

*[VOC sources: "chapter 11 moment," "moment is gone," "Discord quiet by week three," "she's tried six apps"]*

## ✅ SOLUTION SECTION

**Transition line:**
The problem isn't that you need more willpower. It's that the conversation has always been in the wrong place.

**Headline:**
What if the reaction was right there — on the paragraph that prompted it?

**Body:**
Flipbook puts the conversation on the page. Not in a thread below it. Not in a separate app. In the margin, next to the line that someone in your club couldn't read past without saying something.

You create a club the same way you'd start a WhatsApp group. Share one link. Your friends join. You pick a book, open it inside the app, and start reading. As you read, your club's reactions appear in the margins — keyed to the page you're both on. No spoilers of what's ahead. No dead threads from three weeks ago. Just the room, where you are, right now.

That's the thing that's been missing. Not another app with a social layer bolted on. The conversation, finally, in the right place.

*[VOC sources: page-keyed reactions mechanism, WhatsApp-easy framing, magic moment description]*

## ⚡ FEATURES — BENEFIT-LED CALLOUTS

*(Each callout uses exact or near-verbatim brand voice from the product vision doc)*

**Make a club like a group chat.**
Create one in under two minutes. Share a link. Anyone who taps it is in. No scheduling required, no moderating overhead, no snacks.

**Talk on the page — not after it.**
Long-press a paragraph. Drop a reaction. See your club's reactions appear in the margin as they read. The conversation lives where the feeling happens.

**See who's alongside you.**
A soft, ambient view of where your club is in the book. Close enough to feel together. Never close enough to make you feel behind.

**Read anywhere, with no connection.**
Open a book in Flipbook. Read on the train. We'll catch up when you do.

**Follow the writers you love.**
Indie authors publish chapters on Flipbook. When they drop a new one, you'll know — and the room opens.

*[Brand voice sources: "Make a club like a group chat. Invite by link. Read together." / "When a creator drops a chapter, you'll know. The room opens."]*

## ❓ FAQ — OBJECTION HANDLING

*[Objections sourced directly from the VOC pull and brand voice objection handlers in product-vision.md]*

**Q: I have a Goodreads account. How is this different?**
Goodreads is for the books you've finished. Flipbook is for the book you're reading right now. The conversation happens on the page, while the feeling is still there — not in a review you write when it's over.

**Q: I've tried Discord book clubs. They always go quiet.**
Same here. They die because the conversation isn't tied to the book — it's in a general channel that nobody has a reason to come back to. On Flipbook, the reactions live on the specific page. The book pulls you back. The server doesn't have to.

**Q: I prefer reading alone.**
You still can. Solo reading works fine inside the app. The clubs are there when you want them — and most readers find they want them more than they expected.

**Q: I'm not a "big reader."**
Most people who join Flipbook aren't either. They're readers who keep meaning to finish and almost never do. Showing up alongside other people changes how it feels.

**Q: When does beta open?**
We're targeting a closed beta very soon. Waitlist members get access before anyone else. We'll email you the moment your invite is ready.

**Q: What do I need to get started?**
An email address to join the waitlist. A phone when the app is ready — iOS and Android at launch.

## 💡 7 SWEEPS EDITING LOG

### Sweep 1 — Clarity
- "Books die in the gap between intention and finishing" — tested: understood immediately, no jargon
- "Low-grade ambient guilt" — slightly literary, but intentional; matches the brand voice exactly
- All CTAs are one clear action: email → invite code → app

### Sweep 2 — Voice
- Checked every section against brand anti-patterns: no "platform," no "journey," no "reimagined," no exclamation points
- "The room opens" kept — this is the Flipbook brand signature phrase
- FAQ answers written in conversational first-person, not corporate FAQ tone

### Sweep 3 — Proof
- Social proof section left as structured placeholder (no invented testimonials)
- Mechanism explained specifically: reactions appear in the margin, keyed to the page, not ahead of where you are
- Competitive differentiation is specific (names Goodreads and Discord, not "competitors")

### Sweep 4 — Specificity
- "Chapter 11" used instead of "a chapter you love" — specific feels real
- "Under two minutes" for club creation — from the user journey map in the vision doc
- "Week three" for Discord death — verbatim from the competitive analysis

### Sweep 5 — Stickiness
- "Books die in the gap between intention and finishing" — verbatim VOC, sticky because it's exact
- "The moment is gone" — used twice (problem + agitation) for recall
- "The room opens" — repeatable brand phrase

### Sweep 6 — Emotion
- Agitation section targets the exact emotion: the "right now" feeling that never gets expressed
- "Walk past them. You feel it" — present tense, physical detail
- "The version of yourself who finishes" — identity-level desire, not feature desire

### Sweep 7 — Zero (cuts made)
- Removed: "We're passionate about reading" — never earned its place
- Removed: "innovative" and "seamless" — zero information
- Removed: second CTA in how-it-works section — it distracted from the flow
- Tightened: feature callouts from 3 sentences to 2 each

## 💡 VALIDATION & A/B TEST PLAN

### 5-Second Test (run before launch)
**Test these elements:**
Show only the hero (headline + subheadline + CTA) for 5 seconds. Ask:
- "What does this app do?"
- "Who is it for?"
- "What would you do next?"

**Pass criteria:** 80%+ can name "reading with others" and "reactions in the book" without seeing the body copy.

### A/B Tests (post-launch when traffic allows)
| Variable | Version A (control) | Version B (test) | Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| Headline | "You used to finish books. You can again." | "The book club that's always in session." | Email signups |
| CTA button | "Reserve my spot" | "Get my invite code" | Click-through |
| Problem section | Lead with guilt | Lead with the chapter 11 moment | Scroll depth + conversion |
| Qualifier field | "What's the last book you finished?" | No qualifier field | Completion rate vs. signup quality |

### Wynter Test (if budget allows)
Run the hero + problem section with 5 target readers (women 25–40, regular book buyers, have tried Goodreads). Ask:
- "Does this describe a problem you actually have?"
- "What's confusing or unbelievable?"
- "Would you sign up? Why or why not?"

### What 'good' looks like
- Email conversion rate: 15–25% of page visitors (waitlist pages typically 10–20%)
- Qualifier completion rate: 60%+ (optional fields above 50% signal strong engagement)
- 5-second test pass rate: 80%+
